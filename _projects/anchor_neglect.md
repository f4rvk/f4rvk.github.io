When researchers collect data on Likert-type scales and then translate those numerical results into verbal conclusions, they routinely discard the semantic content of the scale anchors that gave the numbers meaning in the first place. This project develops a systematic account of this problem — *anchor neglect* — and argues that it undermines the validity of a wide range of published claims in psychology.

The paper is organized around a claim-type spine that identifies the specific inferential moves where anchor neglect occurs, distinguishing three subtypes: descriptive, inferential, and threshold anchor neglect. This typology doubles as a coding scheme for an audit of published articles in *JPSP*, using a "slide rate" metric to quantify how frequently anchor information is lost in verbal conclusions.

The project also includes a scoping review of existing commentary on scale interpretation problems and a dilemma formulation that clarifies why the issue is not merely cosmetic but poses a genuine threat to construct validity.

This is my comprehensive exam project, with a defense targeted before Fall 2026 and the manuscript aimed at *Perspectives on Psychological Science*.
